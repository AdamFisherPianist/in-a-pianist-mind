What Makes a Piano Sound Like a Piano?

Every piano note you hear is more than just a single frequency. When a hammer strikes a string, it creates a fundamental pitch, but also a rich blend of additional frequencies called harmonics. These harmonics shape the tone, warmth, and character of the sound.

Without harmonics, a piano would sound dull or synthetic. With them, each note becomes full and expressive. This harmonic mix is what separates a piano from a pure sine wave or digital beep.

What Are Harmonics?

When you play a piano note, you're not just hearing one sound. You're hearing a stack of frequencies called harmonics, or overtones. The first harmonic is the note you played — the "fundamental" — but every higher harmonic contributes to the timbre, or color, of the sound.
